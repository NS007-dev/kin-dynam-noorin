Unit Part • Kinematics

Describing motion

In kinematics, we talk about how something moves. We don’t talk about forces yet. We use words and graphs like displacement, velocity, and acceleration.

Key words
Displacement (Δd): change in position (where you end up vs start)
Velocity (v): how fast displacement changes (direction matters)
Acceleration (a): how fast velocity changes
Constant acceleration (SUVAT): v = v₀ + at Δd = v₀t + ½at² v² = v₀² + 2aΔd
Velocity–time graph

Key idea

How kinematics and dynamics connect

Kinematics and dynamics are usually taught separately, but they actually work together. One tells you what the motion looks like, and the other tells you why it happens.

Kinematics: describes motion using velocity, acceleration, time, and displacement.

Dynamics: explains motion using forces and Newton’s laws.

Forces cause acceleration, and acceleration is what changes velocity.
In problems, dynamics is often used first to find acceleration, then kinematics is used to predict motion.
Unit Part • Dynamics

Explaining motion (the “why”)

Dynamics is about forces. The biggest idea is net force. If forces balance out, net force is zero, so acceleration is zero. If forces don’t balance, the object accelerates in the direction of the net force.

Newton’s Laws (simple version): 1) ΣF = 0 → constant velocity (could be 0) 2) ΣF = ma → net force causes acceleration 3) Forces happen in pairs (action-reaction)
Connection: Dynamics helps you find “a” (from ΣF = ma). Then kinematics uses “a” to predict motion.
Common mistakes

Stuff people say that’s wrong

❌ “Force is needed to keep something moving.”
✅ You only need net force to change motion. Constant velocity needs ΣF = 0.

❌ “Bigger mass always means bigger acceleration.”
✅ For the same force, bigger mass means smaller acceleration (a = F/m).

What to notice

Simple patterns

These are the “say it out loud” takeaways.

• More force → more acceleration
• More mass → less acceleration (if force stays the same)
• Acceleration is what changes velocity
Interactive • Free-Body Diagram

Build a free-body diagram

A free-body diagram is just a picture of all the forces on an object. Change the sliders to see how net force changes, and what that means for acceleration.

How to explain an FBD

Simple steps

1) Name the forces (weight, normal, friction, applied)
2) Decide which direction is positive
3) Find net force (what’s bigger?)
4) Use ΣF = ma to decide acceleration direction
If ΣF = 0 → a = 0 → velocity stays the same (could be moving or stopped).
Real-life examples

Where this shows up

These are examples that connect the unit to real life.

Car safety
Seatbelts: Your body wants to keep moving (inertia) when the car stops. The belt is the force that changes your motion so you stop with the car.
Sports (kinesiology)
Landing: bending knees increases the time to stop. More time → smaller acceleration → smaller force on joints.
Buildings
Structures: engineers calculate forces so things don’t fail. If forces are too large, parts can accelerate/move in unsafe ways.
Everyday feeling
“Pushed back” in a car: you’re not really pushed back. Your body resists changing motion, and the seat pushes you forward.
Quick wrap-up

The unit in one sentence

Net force causes acceleration, and acceleration is what changes velocity and motion.

Common situations

Where people get confused

1) “If it’s moving, there must be a force pushing it.”
Not always. If the net force is 0, it can keep moving at a constant velocity. You only need a net force to change motion (speed up, slow down, or turn).

2) “Friction is always the same.”
Friction depends on the surfaces and how hard they’re pressed together. It usually acts opposite the direction of motion (or opposite the way it would move).

3) “Normal force = weight every time.”
Only on a flat surface with no extra vertical forces. If there’s an angle or another force, normal force can be different.
These 3 ideas are basically the reason a lot of dynamics questions feel tricky.
